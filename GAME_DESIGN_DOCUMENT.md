# Fish for President

**Subtitle:** A tiny campaign against a rising tide.  
**Format:** Short browser-based choice narrative with light stat management  
**Target playtime:** 10-15 minutes  
**Target audience:** Teens, educators, competition judges, and players interested in environmental issues

## Premise and Purpose

Carpter, a small fish from the Louisiana coast, enters human politics after pollution, warming water, wetland loss, and repeated delays threaten his home. The campaign begins locally and grows into a national movement.

The fish and campaign are funny, but the crisis is real. The game argues that environmental problems are also political, economic, and moral problems. Its central tension is:

> A fish running for president is ridiculous. The reason it is running is not.

### Tone and Environmental Storytelling

Each chapter combines political pressure with one clear environmental focus. The player should learn through damaged places, affected characters, policy tradeoffs, and one optional short **Campaign Note**, not through long lectures.

Every chapter should include:

- one memorable environmental image
- one political decision connected to that issue
- one heartfelt character moment
- one light comic beat
- no more than one short factual Campaign Note

Humor comes from Carpter navigating human politics, not from making environmental harm seem unimportant.

## Player Experience

The player begins with a short prologue that defines Carpter's background, personality, and approach to problems. These choices quietly shape his starting position and later story callbacks. After the prologue, the game becomes a compact political narrative. Carpter meets advisers, reviews problems, makes promises, negotiates with powerful people, and lives with delayed consequences.

**Core loop:** Receive a situation report -> hear conflicting advice -> choose Carpter's response -> commit to a policy or compromise -> see immediate reactions -> face delayed consequences later.

During the prologue, stat effects remain hidden so choices feel like character decisions rather than optimization. Afterward, the player can view the campaign's condition, but individual choices do not announce exact `+1` or `-1` effects. Consequences should be communicated through dialogue, reports, relationships, and changing conditions.

### Political Paths

Carpter gradually develops one of three political identities. These are hidden role-playing tendencies, not classes selected at the start.

| Path | Core belief | Typical strength | Typical risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Stand With the People** | Build broad coalitions and make change through solidarity | Trust and durable support | Compromise may weaken bold action |
| **Master Their Game** | Use institutions, deals, and opponents' own rules to create change | Funds and political flexibility | Carpter may become too comfortable with the system |
| **Challenge Corporate Power** | Rally ordinary people and confront corporations responsible for environmental harm | Ocean Hope and energized supporters | Aggressive rhetoric may isolate useful allies |

The populist path attacks concentrated corporate power and corruption, not workers, small businesses, or entire groups of people. No path is automatically the good or bad route.

## Core Stats

| Player-facing stat | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Support | How many people believe in the campaign |
| Ocean Hope | How much the campaign actually helps the environment |
| Funds | Resources available for travel, outreach, and organizing |
| Hydration | The fish's health and ability to continue campaigning |

No choice should be purely decorative or perfect. Each choice should reveal a value, political strategy, or environmental tradeoff. Important choices should also affect later scenes or how recurring characters treat Carpter.

Stats stay between 0 and 10. A new game begins at 3 Support, 3 Ocean Hope, 3 Funds, and 5 Hydration before hidden prologue effects are applied.

### Resource Costs

Funds are spent on staff, travel, research, organizing, and carrying out promises. Stronger policies should normally require more Funds.

| Campaign stage | Typical cost | Expected impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chapters 1-2 | Funds -1 | Small local improvement or early campaign gain |
| Chapters 3-4 | Funds -2 | Strong regional policy or major campaign operation |
| Chapters 5-6 | Funds -3 | National plan or election-defining commitment |

A particularly strong deal must either cost more Funds or create a serious political tradeoff. Funds cannot fall below 0. Unaffordable options remain visible but unavailable. The player sees the Funds cost before deciding; other consequences are revealed through reports and reactions.

Required decisions always include a limited no-cost fallback so the story cannot dead-end when Funds reach 0. These fallback actions prevent immediate disaster but rarely create lasting change.

## Campaign Route

| Chapter | Compass | Environmental focus | Political question |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **Home** | Pollution, warming water, and wetland loss | What will Carpter promise, and what will he compromise to protect home? |
| 2 | **Attention** | Plastic pollution and public awareness | Can Carpter turn attention into meaningful support? |
| 3 | **Shared Survival** | Flooding, storms, and climate justice | Who gets protected first when resources are limited? |
| 4 | **Corruption** | Greenwashing and industrial pollution | Expose, negotiate with, or depend on polluters? |
| 5 | **Truth vs Delay** | Climate delay, denial, and lobbying | Can Carpter defend action against polished excuses? |
| 6 | **Change** | Policy, responsibility, and collective action | What kind of change can Carpter actually achieve? |

## Mechanics and Endings

**Political dialogue:** Short conversations lead into decisions. Advisers and opponents disagree, forcing Carpter to choose whose trust to earn or lose.  
**Policy decisions:** Each chapter contains one major commitment with delayed consequences.  
**Relationships and factions:** Recurring characters remember Carpter's promises. Coastal communities, environmental organizers, and business interests react differently to his choices.  
**Political identity:** Major decisions add hidden points toward one of Carpter's three paths. A dominant path unlocks later dialogue and changes the ending's tone, while mixed-path play remains valid.  
**Situation reports:** Brief chapter-opening and chapter-closing summaries show what changed without revealing every hidden number.  
**Debate event:** A three-round political dialogue sequence where earlier policies, evidence, relationships, and Carpter's childhood affect available response cards. Debate answers do not cost Funds; preparation may.  
**Ending calculator:** Final stats, debate performance, corporate dependence, coalition strength, and Hydration select one of five outcomes: President Carpter, Movement Leader, Meme Candidate, Corporate Puppet, or Forgotten Campaign.

The four core stats remain visible. Relationships with coastal communities, environmental organizers, and business interests stay hidden and are communicated through dialogue, support, opposition, and later opportunities.

Each ending also receives a political-path variation. For example, a successful Carpter may govern as a coalition builder, institutional strategist, or people's champion depending on how the player role-played him.

## UI and Art Direction

The game should feel like an interactive pixel-art storybook rather than a complex simulator. Every screen keeps the four stats visible, shows one clear scene image, uses short text blocks, and presents large accessible choice buttons.

The visual palette should combine soft ocean blues and marsh greens with more serious storm grays and industrial colors. Initial art will use simple original pixel placeholders: a fish in a suit, six location backgrounds, stat icons, a title screen, and ending cards. Final art begins only after the full game is playable.

## Scope and Submission Assets

**Minimum playable scope:** title screen, prologue, six campaign turns, four stats, recurring-character relationships, debate sequence, five endings, and responsive browser controls.

**Submission assets:** deployed game link, 3-5 screenshots, optional short gameplay video, student-written concept description and reflection, process log, and works cited for factual environmental claims.

**Production rule:** Prioritize a complete playable story, clear message, and understandable consequences before art, sound, or extra mechanics.
